# The Truth–Knowability–Ethics–Benefit Tetrad

## Abstract

This report tests, rather than simply defends, the thesis that judgments of truth, knowability, moral rightness, and benefit form a single deliberative architecture rather than four ultimately separable domains. The main conclusion is qualified but positive. The **strong** version of the thesis fails: neither the Western canon nor the Islamic tradition supports a flat claim that revelation, reason, sense-experience, and conscience are simply co-equal authorities in every hard case. What survives stress-testing is a **modified** thesis: the four judgments are analytically distinguishable but practically inseparable, and they are integrated through the agent’s intentional orientation—*niyya* / *qaṣd*, Aristotelian choice, Husserlian intentionality, and Kantian disposition—within a tradition-constituted framework. In that stronger and more careful form, the proposal can absorb entity["people","Bertrand Russell","british philosopher"]’s appearance/reality distinction, entity["people","Immanuel Kant","german philosopher"]’s architectonic of theoretical and practical reason, entity["people","Aristotle","ancient greek philosopher"]’s union of right reason and right desire, the *maqāṣid* tradition of entity["people","al-Shatibi","andalusi jurist"], and the heart-centered epistemology of entity["people","al-Ghazali","persian theologian"]—but only by rejecting reductionism, preserving internal pluralism, and building explicit conflict-resolution rules. The most publishable formulation is therefore **not** “the four domains are one thing simpliciter,” but rather “the four domains are four irreducible moments of one deliberative act, whose integration is governed by intention, teleology, and evidence within historically embodied traditions of reasoning.” That formulation is operationalizable in software, but only as a structured deliberation tool, not as a neutral oracle or a fatwā engine. citeturn17view0turn22view0turn27view0turn30view0turn37view3turn10search4turn10search2turn17view8turn41search5

## The fragmentation problem

Modern Western philosophy gained real clarity by separating questions of what is true, what can be known, what ought to be done, and what outcomes matter. In entity["book","The Problems of Philosophy","1912 russell"], Russell distinguishes sense-data from physical objects, knowledge by acquaintance from knowledge by description, and knowledge from “probable opinion,” thereby clarifying why certainty is scarce and why philosophical humility is rational. In entity["book","Critique of Pure Reason","1781 kant"], Kant similarly distinguishes sensibility from understanding and argues that experience requires a priori forms and categories; the well-known line that “thoughts without content are void; intuitions without conceptions, blind” captures a structural dependence between receptivity and conceptuality without collapsing them. These distinctions were genuine achievements, not mistakes. citeturn19view0turn19view1turn19view2turn34view2turn34view4turn34view5

The fragmentation becomes sharper with entity["people","David Hume","scottish philosopher"] and entity["people","G. E. Moore","british philosopher"]. In entity["book","A Treatise of Human Nature","1739 hume"], Hume’s famous complaint is not merely that moralists use the word “ought,” but that they move from “is” to “ought” without explaining how a “new relation or affirmation” has arisen. MacIntyre’s classic reading shows that Hume is subtler than the classroom caricature: Hume is not simply banning moral inference; he is forcing philosophers to say what mediating principle licenses the transition. Moore’s entity["book","Principia Ethica","1903 moore"] then blocks a second shortcut by insisting that “good” is not definable as pleasure, desire-satisfaction, or any other natural property. Together, Hume and Moore do not disprove ethics; they prohibit lazy bridges. citeturn22view0turn22view1turn21view1turn27view0

Those prohibitions helped produce a divided disciplinary map: epistemology handles justification, metaethics handles the status of value, normative ethics handles obligation, and decision theory or consequentialist analysis handles outcomes. The gains are obvious: tighter arguments, cleaner vocabularies, and greater resistance to rhetorical smuggling. The losses are equally obvious: actual agents do not deliberate in four separate rooms. In lived judgment, factual belief, confidence level, moral assessment, and expected harm or benefit enter together. This is the point at which both entity["people","Alasdair MacIntyre","scottish philosopher"] and entity["people","Ludwig Wittgenstein","austrian philosopher"] become central. MacIntyre argues that practical reasoning is tradition-constituted, not tradition-free, while Wittgenstein’s late reflections on certainty suggest that inquiry always already leans on “hinges” or groundless grounds that are not ordinarily up for evidential audit in each individual case. That does not abolish analysis; it explains why analysis alone does not tell a person how to live. citeturn17view8turn17view7turn39search12

The upshot is that the tetrad problem is real. The analytic divisions are intellectually serviceable, but they are not phenomenologically self-sufficient. A theory that cannot explain how agents legitimately move among truth-claims, confidence judgments, moral norms, and impact assessments remains incomplete. The burden of proof, then, is not on integration as such; it is on any integrative model that pretends Hume and Moore never happened. citeturn22view0turn27view0turn17view8

## The integrative architecture in classical Islamic thought

Classical Islamic thought does not treat epistemology, morality, law, spirituality, and social benefit as one undifferentiated mass. It distinguishes them sharply. But it also situates them within a deliberately integrated architecture. The most compact doctrinal expression is the Ḥadīth Jibrīl, preserved in entity["book","Sahih Muslim","hadith collection"] and widely transmitted in pedagogical collections, where *islām*, *īmān*, and *iḥsān* are presented together as dimensions of one religion rather than separate departments. This structure matters philosophically: outward action, doctrinal assent, and perfected inward orientation are not rivals but concentric layers. The famous intentions hadith—*innamā al-aʿmālu bi’l-niyyāt*—makes the same point from the other direction by tying the public meaning of action to the inward orientation that animates it. citeturn42search0turn42search3turn42search11

The Qur’anic lexicon reinforces this compression of domains. The word *ḥaqq* names not only truth in the narrow propositional sense but also reality, right, due claim, and what stands fast against nullity; the verse “the truth has come and falsehood has vanished” is therefore not merely semantic but ontological and normative in force. The Diyanet translation project, in its own preface, also foregrounds a pattern crucial to *maqāṣid* reasoning: revelation restores and confirms the essentials of religion while preserving life, property, lineage, intellect, and religion as core protected goods. That is already an integration of truth, norm, and benefit rather than a bare catalog of commands. citeturn43search5turn45view1

Within legal theory, al-Ghazālī and al-Shāṭibī are the two indispensable anchors. Al-Ghazālī’s *uṣūl* work became part of the canonical architecture through which jurists rank evidences, reason analogically, and identify protected goods; his *Iḥyāʾ* meanwhile ties knowledge to inward purification and moral transformation. Al-Shāṭibī systematizes the hierarchy of *ḍarūriyyāt*, *ḥājiyyāt*, and *taḥsīniyyāt* and treats *maṣlaḥa* not as crude utility but as the intelligible purposiveness of the law. That is the backbone of any benefit-axis analysis here: benefit is never simply “what works,” but what preserves the human goods the law exists to secure. citeturn10search4turn10search0turn9search16turn9search7

What this tradition adds, beyond legal theory narrowly conceived, is a moral epistemology of the heart. In the available English materials on al-Ghazālī’s *Marvels of the Heart*, the *qalb* is not a sentimental faculty but the site where knowledge, attention, susceptibility to illusion, and moral formation intersect. Likewise, English materials on entity["people","Imam Rabbani","mujaddidi sufi"] repeatedly summarize his insistence that knowledge, practice, and purity of intention belong together. In other words, Islamic integrative thought does not merely say “know the rule and then apply it.” It asks what kind of knower the agent has become and whether the knower’s interior state deforms or clarifies judgment. citeturn9search7turn13search1turn13search9

The major internal disagreement is the debate over *al-ḥusn wa-l-qubḥ al-ʿaqliyyān*: whether reason can discern moral goodness and badness, and in what sense. Here the strong synthesis thesis immediately encounters resistance. Muʿtazilī moral rationalism and standard caricatures of Ashʿarī voluntarism are both misleadingly simple. Sherman Jackson’s classic study and more recent work on Ashʿarī reason in ethics show that the field is more complex: some Ashʿarī lines make room for real rational moral discernment while still refusing to place unaided reason on equal footing with revelation in every domain. This is exactly where the report’s final thesis must become asymmetrical rather than flatly co-authoritative. citeturn10search2turn16search1turn10search15

Modern bridge figures make the same point in different idioms. entity["people","Seyyed Hossein Nasr","iranian philosopher"] argues for the re-sacralization of knowledge; entity["people","Taha Abdurrahman","moroccan philosopher"] offers an ethically centered critique of secular reason; entity["people","Wael Hallaq","legal theorist"] interprets Taha as offering an alternative moral anthropology; entity["people","Recep Senturk","turkish sociologist"] frames Islamic civilization in terms of multiplexity and open civilization. None of these thinkers erases internal pluralism. Their shared value lies in showing that an integrated epistemic-moral framework is not an antiquarian invention but a live conceptual project. citeturn10search3turn43search10turn43search14turn11search1turn11search3turn11search19

## Western anchors and the bridge concept of intention

The most useful Western anchors for the synthesis are not the theories that try to reduce all judgment to one dimension, but the theories that preserve plurality while still insisting on practical unity. Aristotle is decisive here. In entity["book","Nicomachean Ethics","aristotle"], human good is “activity of soul in accordance with virtue,” and good choice requires both true reasoning and right desire. Later, in Book VI, *phronēsis* is described as the capacity to deliberate well about what conduces to the good life in general, including particulars. That already fuses epistemic, moral, and prudential considerations without flattening them into one variable. citeturn37view0turn37view3

Kant contributes something different and equally indispensable. In entity["book","Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals","1785 kant"], a good will is good not because of what it effects but “simply by virtue of the volition,” and an action has moral worth only when done “for the sake of the law.” The categorical imperative universalizes maxims, while the humanity formulation requires treating persons always as ends. Kant therefore blocks the reduction of the moral axis to the benefit axis. Yet in the entity["book","Critique of Practical Reason","1788 kant"], he also reconnects moral reason to a wider architectonic by arguing that the *summum bonum* requires postulates—especially God and immortality—not as speculative proofs but as practical necessities of moral intelligibility. Kant does not collapse truth into ethics; he reopens traffic between them. citeturn30view0turn31view1turn31view2turn31view3turn31view5

Russell supplies the epistemic sobriety the framework needs. His distinction between acquaintance and description, and his insistence that much of what passes as knowledge is only probable opinion, prevent any theological or ethical system from pretending to certainty it does not possess. This aligns more closely with the Islamic distinction between *yaqīn* and *ẓann* than might first appear. Likewise, the later Wittgensteinian reminder that some certainties function as enabling commitments rather than inferred conclusions helps explain why revelation-grounded or tradition-grounded moral worlds can be rationally operative without being reconstructed from neutral premises each time. citeturn19view1turn19view2turn17view7turn39search12

The most direct analytic bridge to Islamic *niyya* is entity["people","Edmund Husserl","german philosopher"]’s doctrine of intentionality. Husserl’s central claim is that conscious acts are always “of” or “about” something; they are directed. This is not identical to moral intention. Husserlian intentionality is formal *aboutness*, whereas *niyya* is purposive and normatively charged. But the comparison is still philosophically fruitful because both treat the act as internally structured by what it is for and toward. This comparison is, to the best of available scholarship, more inferential than documentary; the literature directly joining *niyya* to Husserl remains thin. Still, as a bridge concept it is more precise than vague appeals to “holism.” citeturn41search1turn41search5turn41search7turn42search0

That yields the report’s central bridge claim: intention is the hinge because it binds belief, evidence, norm, and outcome at the level of agency. A true proposition can be weaponized by a vicious intention; a beneficial result can be morally compromised by corruption of purpose; and even the pursuit of certainty can become epistemically distorted if the knower is attached to a favored conclusion. The tetrad therefore hangs together, not because all four judgments are the same, but because they are all filtered through the purposive orientation of an acting knower. citeturn31view3turn37view3turn41search5turn42search11

## A unified but asymmetrical framework

The strongest defensible version of the tetrad thesis is this: **truth, knowability, ethics, and benefit are four irreducible moments of one deliberative act**. They are not reducible to one another, but in serious practical reasoning none can be responsibly isolated from the others. This “irreducible unity” formula is stronger than interdisciplinarity and weaker than metaphysical identity. It preserves the analytic gains of Hume and Moore while recovering the practical unity visible in Aristotle, Kant, MacIntyre, and the Islamic *uṣūl* tradition. citeturn22view0turn27view0turn37view3turn31view3turn17view8turn10search4

The framework’s second claim is about sources. The prompt’s working thesis proposed revelation, reason, experience, and conscience as “co-authoritative.” That form is too strong. Once hard conflicts arise, all traditions need meta-rules. Classical Islamic legal theory already provides such rules: authenticity, generality/specificity, abrogation or non-abrogation, analogy, public interest, and the status of custom and harm are not optional add-ons but part of the method. The better formulation is therefore: **the four sources are mutually necessary but asymmetrically ordered**. Revelation provides the highest-order normative horizon inside an Islamic framing when its relevance and interpretive force are established; reason adjudicates coherence, analogy, and non-contradiction; experience supplies empirical predicates and consequence-tracking; conscience diagnoses sincerity, moral residue, and self-deception, but is never self-authenticating. citeturn10search4turn10search0turn10search2turn16search1turn17view9

That asymmetry matters because it prevents two opposite failures. It blocks a secular reduction in which revelation is merely one more preference among others; it also blocks a voluntarist reduction in which empirical reality, rational contradiction, and moral phenomenology carry no authority once a text has been invoked. The surviving synthesis is thus neither liberal proceduralism nor simple command theory. It is closer to a tradition-constituted practical realism, with MacIntyre furnishing the Western metatheoretical parallel and the *maqāṣid* tradition furnishing the Islamic grammar of ends. citeturn17view8turn15search1turn11search5turn10search4

This yields six falsifiable commitments:

1. A judgment of moral rightness that ignores truth-conditions is defective.  
2. A truth-claim pressed into action while its evidential status remains weak must be marked as *ẓann*, not *yaqīn*.  
3. A benefit assessment cannot by itself define the good; it can only inform or instantiate it.  
4. Intention is always morally relevant, though not always sufficient to justify an act.  
5. Source conflicts must be resolved by public rules, not charismatic intuition.  
6. Persistent qualified disagreement is itself a meaningful output, not an error state. citeturn19view2turn27view0turn31view3turn10search4turn17view8

If those commitments are accepted, then the tetrad becomes a rigorous framework rather than a slogan. If one insists either that only descriptive evidence counts, or that only command counts, or that only consequences count, then the framework collapses. That vulnerability is not a weakness; it is what makes the thesis falsifiable. citeturn22view0turn27view0turn31view5

## Stress tests and disconfirmation results

The first major stress test is Hume and Moore together. Here the framework does **not** bridge the is/ought gap by deduction, and it does **not** define good in terms of benefit. Hume remains right that an “ought” introduces something not contained in a chain of “is” statements unless a mediating principle is supplied. Moore remains right that benefit, pleasure, advantage, or desire-satisfaction cannot simply be identical with goodness. The framework survives only by conceding both points and then arguing that practical deliberation always starts from thicker premises than bare description: teleological, covenantal, or tradition-embedded premises about what human beings are for. In short, the framework answers Hume and Moore by **changing the starting point**, not by refuting their formal objections. citeturn22view0turn21view1turn27view0turn10search4turn37view3

The second stress test is the Islamic Euthyphro problem. The Ashʿarī/Muʿtazilī disagreement does not disappear inside a rhetoric of “co-authority.” Sherman Jackson’s work and recent reassessments of Ashʿarī ethics show that one can defend a more rationally serious Ashʿarism than older orientalist caricatures allowed, but the disagreement remains real. Ramon Harvey’s Māturīdī engagement with MacIntyre shows another path, one more open to natural-law style reasoning under divine wisdom. The finding here is negative but clarifying: **the framework cannot be metaethically neutral**. Any operational version must declare whether it is running in a command-centered, wisdom-centered, or natural-law-leaning mode. citeturn10search2turn16search1turn17view9turn15search1

The third stress test is benefit reasoning under real contemporary disagreement. The best documented case retrieved here is organ donation. The Fiqh Council of North America’s 2018 position, as summarized by Aasim Padela and Jasser Auda, judges organ donation morally permissible in principle under conditions including first-person authorization and specific death criteria, but the broader Islamic bioethical literature also records substantive disagreement over brain death, bodily inviolability, and the scope of public benefit. This is precisely the kind of case that shows both the strength and limit of the framework: *maqāṣid* reasoning is indispensable, but it does not generate one uncontested answer from identical inputs. Therefore the application must surface disagreement rather than hide it. citeturn13search2turn13search10turn13search18

The fourth stress test is temporal or hermeneutic instability—what the outline called the *mansūkh* / stability problem. The open materials reviewed here support a more modest claim than a full abrogation study would require. The Diyanet preface explicitly distinguishes enduring essentials from historically varying rulings across prophetic dispensations, while the *maqāṣid* tradition supplies a language of stable ends beneath changing applications. The framework can therefore handle some legal-temporal variation **if** it distinguishes invariant ends from historically indexed enactments. But it does not independently solve every abrogation dispute. A full Qurʾānic *naskh* chapter would require more direct engagement with classical usul manuals than was retrievable in uniformly searchable form here. citeturn45view1turn10search4turn10search0

The fifth stress test is AI personhood. The literature retrieved here is still emerging. The recent Cambridge materials on Islam and AI, together with Ebrahim Moosa’s work on Muslim theological encounters with science, show that AI raises unresolved questions about human uniqueness, moral status, technological mediation, and the difference between intellect and heart. To the best of available scholarship, there is **no** stable Sunni or cross-traditional scholarly consensus on machine personhood. This is therefore a case where the framework can structure inquiry—What is the entity? What claims about consciousness are true? What can actually be known? Which *maqāṣid* are implicated? What harms follow from anthropomorphizing or under-protecting?—but it cannot yet issue a settled verdict. citeturn14search2turn14search5turn14search17

The overall disconfirmation result is therefore mixed. The descriptive claim that the four axes are intertwined in practice survives. The strong source claim fails. The publishable revision is: **integrated deliberation, yes; flat source equality, no; operationalization, yes; final convergence on every hard case, no.** citeturn17view8turn10search4turn13search2turn14search17

## Operationalization and application architecture

The procedure below is specific enough to drive a moral-dilemma analysis tool. Its output is not a verdict; it is a structured deliberation record.

The first step is **case-framing**. The tool asks for the agents, the available options, the affected parties, timing constraints, reversibility, legal constraints, and the user’s role. This is where Russell’s probable-opinion warning matters: the system must distinguish hard facts, assumptions, testimony, and speculation before any norm is applied. citeturn19view2turn17view0

The second step is **tetrad separation**. The system forces the user to state, separately:  
(a) truth-claims (“What is the world like here?”),  
(b) knowability-claims (“How well established is each factual claim?”),  
(c) moral claims (“What duties, prohibitions, rights, virtues, or wrongs are in play?”), and  
(d) benefit claims (“Who is helped, harmed, preserved, burdened, or degraded?”).  
This is the anti-confusion step. It borrows from Hume and Moore by forbidding premature collapse of one axis into another. citeturn22view0turn27view0

The third step is **source mapping**. Each claim is tagged to one or more sources: revelation, reason, experience, conscience. The rules are asymmetrical:

| Source | Typical role | Override conditions | Failure mode to avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revelation (*waḥy*) | Settled obligations, prohibitions, protected goods, moral horizon | Requires authenticity, relevance, and competent interpretation | Proof-texting without usul |
| Reason (*ʿaql*) | Coherence, analogy, impossibility checks, ranking principles | Cannot negate settled text by preference, but can limit false readings | Rationalism detached from norm |
| Experience (*tajriba*) | Facts, probabilities, causal pathways, feasibility, medical/technical data | Decisive wherever the question is empirical | Moralizing without evidence |
| Conscience / heart (*qalb*, *ḍamīr*) | Sincerity check, moral residue, self-deception detection, motivational honesty | Needs formation; cannot stand alone against public reasons | Subjectivism or scrupulosity |

This table is the key operational revision of the original thesis: the sources are all necessary, but not all play the same role in every kind of question. citeturn10search4turn10search2turn16search1turn17view9

The fourth step is **maqāṣid analysis**. The tool asks which protected goods are implicated and at what level: *ḍarūriyyāt* (essentials), *ḥājiyyāt* (needs), or *taḥsīniyyāt* (refinements). The point is not to turn *maqāṣid* into a spreadsheet, but to force explicit ranking. It should also distinguish *maṣlaḥa* from mere preference and from mere market utility. citeturn10search4turn10search0

The fifth step is **intention analysis**. The prompt here must be unusually concrete: “What are you actually trying to secure?” “What outcome would tempt you to misdescribe the facts?” “Would your intention remain the same if recognition, gain, or fear were removed?” This is where the hadith of intention, Kant’s insistence on acting for the sake of the law, and Aristotelian concern for choice converge. The tool should record intended aim, mixed motives, foreseeable corruptions, and moral residue if every option is costly. citeturn31view3turn37view3turn42search11

The sixth step is **confidence and disagreement classification**. The output should not say simply “permissible” or “impermissible” unless the matter is highly settled. It should classify the case as:  
**Settled** – broad consensus on facts and norm;  
**Qualified disagreement** – disagreement among recognized scholars or traditions with shared premises;  
**Open** – disputed facts, unstable criteria, or genuinely underdeveloped literature.  
This is the epistemic humility layer demanded by both Russell and Wittgenstein. citeturn19view2turn17view7turn39search12

The seventh step is **deliberation output**. The user receives a structured dossier containing: the case facts, the tetrad map, the source map, the *maqāṣid* ranking, the intention profile, the confidence class, and a short note on where consultation with scholars, clinicians, lawyers, or affected parties is still needed. The system deliberately refuses to issue fatwās, refuses false certainty, and refuses to hide qualified disagreement behind a single confidence-colored button. citeturn13search2turn14search17turn17view8

As an application architecture, the tool should implement the following design principles. The user journey begins with free-text entry, then guided decomposition, then staged reflection, then a report export. Each explanatory module should be short and sourced: a *maqāṣid* explainer cites al-Shāṭibī and a modern reference; an intentions explainer cites the relevant hadith and cross-traditional parallels; a “certainty” explainer cites Russell and Wittgenstein. Every claim displayed to the user should expose a primary source citation. The interface should be bilingual English/Turkish with Arabic technical terms retained on first use. And the system should explicitly state what it does **not** do: it does not replace qualified scholarly consultation, does not produce legal rulings on contested matters, and does not convert moral uncertainty into simulated objectivity. citeturn10search4turn42search11turn17view0turn17view7turn11search3

## Limitations, working bibliography, and Turkish executive summary

**Limitations and future work.** Three limitations are decisive. First, some required Arabic primary texts—especially for a fully documented *tafsīr* apparatus, for al-Rāghib, for al-Rāzī’s *al-Maḥṣūl*, and for a fully critical treatment of *naskh*—were not uniformly available in searchable open editions during this session, so parts of the Islamic textual base here are stronger on widely translated anchors than on exhaustive primary citation. Second, the Nursi and Sirhindī materials retrieved were sufficient to confirm the knowledge–intention–action nexus, but not to produce a complete passage-level analysis of the Eleventh, Twenty-Third, and Thirtieth Words. Third, the AI personhood literature is still too early-stage for confidence beyond a structured map of open questions. These are not fatal defects for a preprint substrate, but they should be corrected before journal submission. citeturn45view0turn45view1turn14search2turn14search17

**Türkçe yönetici özeti**

Bu raporun temel sorusu şudur: İnsan bir önerme veya eylemle karşılaştığında verdiği dört hüküm—“Bu doğru mu?”, “Bunu bilebilir miyim?”, “Bu ahlâken doğru mu?”, “Bu faydalı mı?”—gerçekten ayrı alanlara mı aittir, yoksa tek bir muhakeme eyleminin farklı yüzleri midir? Araştırmanın vardığı sonuç şudur: Bu dört alan analitik olarak ayrılabilir; fakat fiilî karar verme sürecinde birbirinden bağımsız işletilemez. Güçlü biçimiyle tez, yani vahiy, akıl, tecrübe ve vicdanın her durumda tam anlamıyla eşit ve yatay “eş-otoriteler” olduğu iddiası ikna edici değildir. Buna karşılık daha güçlü ve yayınlanabilir görünen formülasyon şudur: Doğruluk (*ḥaqq / ṣidq*), bilinebilirlik (*ʿilm / yaqīn / ẓann*), ahlâkî değer (*ḥusn / qubḥ*), ve maslahat/zarar (*maṣlaḥa / mafsada*) aynı deliberatif eylemin dört indirgenemez boyutudur; bunları birleştiren asıl mihver ise failin yönelişi, yani niyetidir (*niyya / qaṣd*). citeturn17view0turn22view0turn27view0turn10search4turn42search11

Batı düşüncesi bakımından rapor, ayrıştırmanın boşuna yapılmadığını vurguluyor. Russell, görünen ile gerçek olanı; tanışıklık yoluyla bilgi ile tasvir yoluyla bilgiyi; bilgi ile “muhtemel kanaat”i ayırarak epistemik tevazuyu kuruyor. Kant, *a priori* yapıların ve kategorilerin deneyim için zorunlu olduğunu, ama aynı zamanda ahlâkın sonuçlara indirgenemeyeceğini, iyi iradenin değerinin sonuçta değil iradenin kendisinde bulunduğunu savunuyor. Hume, “is/ought” geçişini açıklamadan yapan ahlâkçıları eleştiriyor; Moore ise “iyi”nin haz, arzu veya fayda ile tanımlanamayacağını söylüyor. Bu iki uyarı, bu rapor için belirleyici: Çerçeve, Hume ve Moore’u çürütmüyor; aksine onların yasağını kabul edip, ahlâkî muhakemenin zaten çıplak olgudan değil daha kalın (*teleological*) öncüllerden başladığını savunuyor. Yani geçerli sentez, “olandan olması gerekene mantıksal türetim” değil; “insanın ne için var olduğuna dair zaten benimsenmiş bir maksat ufku içinde muhakeme”dir. citeturn19view2turn31view3turn22view0turn27view0turn31view5

İslam düşüncesi bakımından en önemli bulgu, klasik geleneğin bu dört alanı “ayırt etmeden karıştırdığı” değil; tersine bilinçli şekilde ayırıp sonra tekrar bütünlediğidir. Ḥadīth Jibrīl’de *islām*, *īmān* ve *iḥsān* birbirinden farklı ama tek dinin iç içe halkaları olarak sunulur. Niyet hadisi, eylemin ahlâkî değerinin dış görünüşten ibaret olmadığını açıkça bildirir. Kur’ân’daki *ḥaqq* kelimesi de sadece önermesel “doğru”yu değil, gerçeklik, haklılık ve yerli yerindelik boyutlarını birlikte taşır. Böylece İslamî yapı, doğruluk-bilgi-ahlâk-fayda ayrımını inkâr etmeden bunları tek bir ontolojik-ahlâkî mimari içinde toplar. Ancak bu bütünlük, düz bir eşitlik modeli şeklinde değildir. Usûl-i fıkıh, deliller arasında sıralama, tahsis, illet, kıyas, maslahat ve yorum kuralları kurar; yani “hangi kaynak ne durumda nasıl konuşur?” sorusuna usûlî cevap verir. citeturn42search0turn42search11turn43search5turn45view1turn10search4

Bu mimarinin benefit/zarar eksenindeki omurgası, Gazzâlî ve özellikle Şâtıbî’de sistemleşen *maqāṣid al-sharīʿa* yaklaşımıdır. Zarûriyyât, hâciyyât ve taḥsîniyyât ayrımı faydayı basit pragmatik kazançtan ayırır. *Maṣlaḥa*, “işe yarayan şey” değildir; din, can, akıl, nesil ve mal gibi korunması gereken temel insanî/şerʿî iyilerin muhafazasına yönelen teleolojik bir kavramdır. Bu nedenle “faydalı olan iyi olandır” denemez; ama fayda değerlendirmesi, ahlâkî hükmün ayrılmaz bir parçasıdır. Bu da Moore’a verilmiş İslamî cevap sayılabilir: “iyi” maslahata indirgenmez, fakat maslahat “iyi”nin dünyevî tezahürlerinden biri olarak vazgeçilmezdir. citeturn10search4turn10search0turn27view0

Raporun en önemli kavramsal köprüsü niyettir. Aristoteles’te iyi seçimin şartı doğru akıl ve doğru arzudur; Kant’ta ahlâkî değer, eylemin yasaya uygun olmasından değil yasa uğruna yapılmasından doğar; Husserl’de bilinç her zaman bir şeye yönelmişlik olarak anlaşılır; hadislerde ise amellerin değeri niyetle tayin edilir. Bu dördünü bire bir özdeş saymak hatalı olur. Husserlci intentionality, ahlâkî niyetten daha biçimseldir; Kantçı *Gesinnung* tek tek niyetlerden daha derin bir karakter/yönelim katmanı ifade eder. Buna rağmen hepsinin ortaklaştığı nokta şudur: Eylem, bilgi ve değer yargısı, failin neye yöneldiğinden bağımsız anlaşılamaz. Bu nedenle niyet, çerçevenin merkez düğümüdür. Doğru bir bilgiyi kötü niyetle kullanmak epistemik-moral deformasyondur; iyi sonuç doğuran bir eylem bozuk niyetle yapılmışsa ahlâkî kusur taşır; bir şeyi “bilmek isteme” biçimimiz bile çoğu zaman niyetle şekillenir. citeturn37view3turn31view3turn41search5turn42search11

Bununla birlikte rapor, bütün gerilimlerin çözüldüğünü iddia etmiyor. En kritik iç gerilim, *al-ḥusn wa-l-qubḥ al-ʿaqliyyān* tartışmasıdır. Muʿtezile, ahlâkî değerin akılla kavranabilir olduğunu çok daha güçlü biçimde savunur; klasik Aşʿarî çizgi ise bunu daha sınırlı veya farklı bir şekilde kabul eder. Son dönem İngilizce literatür, Aşʿarîliği kaba bir “çıplak iradecilik” olarak okumanın yanlış olduğunu gösteriyor; yine de ihtilaf devam ediyor. Bu yüzden geliştirilecek yazılım ya da teorik model “metaetik olarak nötr” davranamaz. En azından hangi kipte çalıştığını belirtmelidir: emir-merkezli mi, hikmet-merkezli mi, tabiî hukuk eğilimli mi? Raporun burada vardığı sonuç şu: Güçlü “dört kaynak eşit otoritedir” tezi başarısızdır; yerine, “kaynaklar birlikte zorunludur ama rolleri asimetriktir” tezi geçirilmelidir. Vahiy normatif ufku kurar; akıl tutarlılık ve kıyas alanında vazgeçilmezdir; tecrübe olgusal sorularda belirleyicidir; vicdan ise samimiyet ve ahlâkî kalıntı tespitinde önemlidir ama tek başına nihai ölçüt değildir. citeturn10search2turn16search1turn17view9

Elde edilen literatür, İslam ve yapay zekâ tartışmasının hızla büyüdüğünü, fakat makine kişi oluşu konusunda yerleşik bir icmâ bulunmadığını gösteriyor. Son hüküm şu şekilde özetlenebilir. Tezin kuvvetli, sade ve yayınlanabilir biçimi şudur: Doğruluk, bilinebilirlik, ahlâkîlik ve maslahat bağımsız disiplinler olarak incelenebilir; ancak failin somut muhakemesinde bunlar tek bir yönelimin farklı ve indirgenemez yüzleridir. Bunu birleştiren asıl kavram niyettir. Fakat bu birliğin çalışması için kaynaklar arasında asimetrik ama kamuya açık kurallar gerekir; ayrıca nitelikli ihtilafın da meşru bir sonuç olduğu kabul edilmelidir.
